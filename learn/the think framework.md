I think you're looking for something much deeper than a note template.

You're looking for a **thinking framework**.

If you ask the same questions every time you learn a new concept, your brain starts building knowledge automatically.

I use something like this:

---

# The THINK Framework

Whenever you learn **any concept**, ask these questions in order.

```text
1. WHAT is it?

2. WHY does it exist?

3. WHAT problem does it solve?

4. HOW does it work?

5. WHERE is it used?

6. WHEN is it used?

7. WHO uses it?

8. WHAT is it connected to?

9. WHAT is similar?

10. WHAT is different?

11. WHAT would happen if it didn't exist?

12. WHAT are common mistakes?

13. WHAT are real examples?

14. HOW would I explain it to a child?

15. CAN I teach it without looking?
```

These 15 questions work for almost everything.

---

# Example: Freedom

Instead of memorizing:

> Freedom = Article 19–22

Think like this.

```markdown
# Freedom

## 1. What is it?

The right to live and express myself without unnecessary government interference.

---

## 2. Why does it exist?

Because people cannot live in a democracy without freedom.

---

## 3. What problem does it solve?

Government oppression.

No free speech.

No movement.

No association.

---

## 4. How does it work?

The Constitution gives citizens specific freedoms.

---

## 5. Where is it used?

Daily life.

Speech.

Travel.

Business.

---

## 6. When is it used?

Whenever a person's liberty is affected.

---

## 7. Who uses it?

Every citizen.

---

## 8. Connected to

[[Democracy]]

[[Article 19]]

[[Article 21]]

[[Fundamental Rights]]

---

## 9. Similar to

Equality

---

## 10. Different from

Constitutional Remedies

---

## 11. What if it didn't exist?

Government could silence anyone.

---

## 12. Common mistake

People think freedom is unlimited.

It isn't.

---

## 13. Real example

Freedom of speech.

---

## 14. Explain to a child

You are allowed to speak your thoughts, but you can't hurt others.

---

## 15. Teach it

Explain Freedom in one minute.
```

Notice how one concept becomes something you understand from many angles.

# I would simplify it even more

Eventually I only use **8 questions**.

```text
1. What is it?

2. Why?

3. How?

4. What problem does it solve?

5. What is it connected to?

6. What is similar/different?

7. Give a real example.

8. Can I explain it without notes?
```

These cover almost everything.

# One more question (my favorite)

Every time you learn something ask:

> **"If I had to invent this myself, why would I invent it?"**

For example

Freedom

↓

Why would I invent Freedom?

↓

Because governments can abuse power.

↓

So Freedom protects citizens.

Now you're reasoning instead of memorizing.

## One final improvement

I would add one question at the very top because it changes the way you learn:

> **"What is the one big idea?"**

Every note should begin with that question.

For example:

- **Freedom** → _People need liberty to live in a democracy._
    
- **Parliament** → _A democracy needs a body to make laws._
    
- **Constituent Assembly** → _India needed a group to design its Constitution._
    
- **Judiciary** → _Someone must interpret the law and resolve disputes._
    

If you can answer **"What is the one big idea?"** in one sentence, you've identified the concept's core. Everything else—facts, articles, dates, examples, and comparisons—becomes supporting information instead of disconnected details. I think that's the single most useful habit you can build for creating a true "thinking" vault in Obsidian.
