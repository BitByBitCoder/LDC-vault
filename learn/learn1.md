# I think your Obsidian should answer only four questions

Every time you create a note, ask:

```text
What is it?

↓

Why does it exist?

↓

What is it connected to?

↓

How can I use it?
```

If your note answers those four questions, you're building understanding, not just storage.

# The idea that changed everything for me

I think you were trying to build a **library**.

But a library stores books.

A brain doesn't store books.

A brain builds **relationships**.

So don't ask:

> "Where should I file this note?"

Ask:

> **"What idea does this belong to, and what ideas does it strengthen?"**

That single question changes how you structure everything.

## One final idea

Based on everything we've discussed over the last few days, I think your goal isn't actually to become good at Obsidian.

Your goal is to become someone who **thinks like an expert**.

Experts don't have better memories because they memorize more. They have better memories because their knowledge is **organized into meaningful networks**.

If you use Obsidian to build those networks—concept by concept, connection by connection—the software stops being a note-taking app and becomes an external extension of your thinking. That's a much more powerful objective than simply trying to remember more facts.
