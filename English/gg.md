Fine. Ranked by actual evidence strength, not vibes:

## Tier 1 — These two are non-negotiable, backed by the strongest research (Dunlosky et al.'s major review of study techniques rated these "high utility," most others "low")

**1. Retrieval practice (testing yourself)** Not rereading notes. Not highlighting. Closing the book and forcing your brain to produce the answer, then checking. This has the largest, most consistently replicated effect on long-term retention of any study technique studied. If you do nothing else right, do this.

**2. Distributed/spaced practice** Same total hours spread across more sessions over more days beats cramming those hours into fewer, longer sessions. Not "review before you forget" — review is more effective _after_ some forgetting has started, which is why waiting a day or two before retesting works better than reviewing same-day.

## Tier 2 — Real effect, smaller than Tier 1

**3. Interleaving (your spiral idea)** Genuine but moderate evidence. Works best for material where you need to _distinguish_ between similar things under pressure (which grammar rule applies, which math formula to use) — which is exactly your exam format. But it's a booster on top of Tier 1, not a replacement for it. If you interleave without retrieval and spacing, you're just switching topics for no reason.

**4. Practice under real exam conditions (timed past papers)** This isn't really a separate "technique" — it's retrieval practice done at maximum realism. Highest-value activity in your final 2 weeks specifically.

## Tier 3 — Low or no evidence, stop doing these if you're doing them

- Rereading notes/textbook
- Highlighting/underlining
- Making things "look organized" (color-coding, pretty notes) — feels productive, doesn't move the needle
- Passive video-watching of explanations without testing yourself after

## The blunt truth about your "Elon Musk method"

What you call frequency-data-driven content selection isn't a _study_ method — it's a _content prioritization_ method. It's smart (why study rare constitutional articles when only 25 ever appeared), but it answers "what to study," not "how to study it." Your actual studying, once you've picked the content, still needs to be retrieval + spacing to work. You could frequency-rank the perfect topic list and still fail if you just reread it 5 times instead of testing yourself on it.

## What this means for your system, concretely

Don't build an elaborate spiral schedule as the centerpiece. Build this instead:

1. Pick topic (frequency-ranked — you're already good at this)
2. Learn it once, actively (not reread — reconstruct from memory immediately after first exposure, which you already do)
3. Test yourself on it 1 day later
4. Test yourself on it again 3-4 days later
5. Mix it into cumulative tests with other topics as you go (this is where interleaving earns its keep)
6. Final 2 weeks: timed full mocks, nothing else

The spiral calendar you designed is a fine _container_ for this — but the content inside each box has to be retrieval-based, or the container doesn't matter.
