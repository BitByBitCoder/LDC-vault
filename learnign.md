Absolutely. If your goal is **to become an expert learner**, I wouldn't start with study techniques. I'd start by understanding **how the brain learns**, then build from there.

Here's a roadmap that moves from beginner to advanced. Think of it like learning programming: you don't start with frameworks—you first understand variables, functions, and data structures.

---

# Phase 1 — Understand How Learning Works (Foundation)

**Goal:** Learn what actually happens when you learn.

### Topics

- What is learning?
    
- Working memory vs. long-term memory
    
- How memories are formed
    
- Why we forget
    
- Encoding, storage, retrieval
    
- Cognitive load
    
- Attention and focus
    

**Questions to ask**

- Why do I forget things?
    
- Why does rereading feel effective?
    
- Why can't I remember after one day?
    
- Why does sleep matter?
    

**Outcome**  
You'll stop blaming your memory and start understanding the learning process.

---

# Phase 2 — Evidence-Based Learning Techniques

**Goal:** Learn methods supported by research.

### Topics

- Active Recall
    
- Spaced Repetition
    
- Retrieval Practice
    
- Interleaving
    
- Desirable Difficulties
    
- Elaboration
    
- Generation Effect
    
- Dual Coding
    

**Questions**

- Why is active recall powerful?
    
- When should I use flashcards?
    
- When should I solve problems?
    
- When should I review?
    

**Outcome**  
You'll replace inefficient habits with more effective ones.

---

# Phase 3 — Build Deep Understanding

**Goal:** Learn concepts instead of memorizing isolated facts.

### Topics

- Mental Models
    
- Schemas
    
- Higher-Order Learning
    
- Conceptual Learning
    
- Pattern Recognition
    
- Analogies
    
- First Principles Thinking
    

**Questions**

- How do experts think?
    
- How do I connect ideas?
    
- How do I build understanding?
    
- Why do some people learn faster?
    

**This is where Justin Sung's work fits best.**

---

# Phase 4 — Organize Knowledge

**Goal:** Make information easier to retrieve and use.

### Topics

- Knowledge Networks
    
- Concept Maps
    
- Hierarchies
    
- Chunking
    
- Compression
    
- Knowledge Graphs
    

Instead of this:

```
Article 14
Article 15
Article 16
Article 17
```

Think like this:

```
Equality
    ├── Article 14
    ├── Article 15
    ├── Article 16
    └── Article 17
```

**Outcome**  
Information becomes easier to remember because it has structure.

---

# Phase 5 — Learn Different Subjects Differently

Different kinds of knowledge need different approaches.

|Subject|Main Focus|
|---|---|
|Math|Problem solving and practice|
|Constitution|Concepts + facts|
|History|Cause and effect, timelines|
|Biology|Systems and relationships|
|Programming|Building projects and debugging|
|Languages|Listening, speaking, reading, writing|

One study method doesn't fit every subject.

---

# Phase 6 — Improve Your Thinking

Now you're learning how to think, not just what to think.

### Topics

- Critical Thinking
    
- Logical Fallacies
    
- Scientific Reasoning
    
- Systems Thinking
    
- Decision Making
    
- Probabilistic Thinking
    

---

# Phase 7 — Learn How Experts Learn

Study people who have developed and explained effective learning systems.

Some good starting points include:

- Barbara Oakley
    
- Justin Sung
    
- Scott H. Young
    
- Anders Ericsson
    

Don't copy everything they say. Compare their ideas with evidence and your own experience.

---

# Phase 8 — Build Your Personal Learning System

Create a repeatable process you can use for any topic.

For example:

```
Preview
    ↓
Understand
    ↓
Connect ideas
    ↓
Retrieve from memory
    ↓
Practice
    ↓
Review after a delay
    ↓
Apply to new situations
```

The exact steps can vary by subject, but the idea is to have a system rather than studying randomly.

---

# Phase 9 — Optimize Your Brain

Learning depends on more than study techniques.

Pay attention to:

- Sleep
    
- Exercise
    
- Nutrition
    
- Stress management
    
- Breaks
    
- Environment
    
- Focus and distraction control
    

---

# Phase 10 — Continuous Improvement

After each week, reflect on questions like:

- What worked well?
    
- What didn't?
    
- What took too long?
    
- What was easy to remember?
    
- Where did I get stuck?
    
- How can I adjust my approach?
    

This habit of evaluating your own learning is called **metacognition**.

## My suggestion for you

Since I know you're preparing for competitive exams like **LDC** while also trying to improve _how_ you learn, I would **not** try to consume dozens of videos at once. Instead, spend about **30–45 minutes each day** studying learning science, then immediately apply one idea to your Constitution or math studies. Learning about learning is most effective when it's practiced right away.

Over time, you'll build a study system that is both efficient and tailored to the subjects you're actually studying, rather than relying on long hours and repetition alone.
