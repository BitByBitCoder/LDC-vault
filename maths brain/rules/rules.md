

Many students try to memorize formulas, but they never learn the rules that allow you to move things around an equation.

Think of algebra as a game with a few simple rules.

# Rule 1: Whatever you do to one side, do to the other

If:

[  
x + 5 = 12  
]

You want to remove the +5.

Subtract 5 from BOTH sides:

[  
x + 5 - 5 = 12 - 5  
]

[  
x = 7  
]

You are not "moving 5 across".

You are subtracting 5 from both sides.

---

# Rule 2: Multiplication and division are opposites

If:

[  
3x = 12  
]

Divide both sides by 3:

[  
\frac{3x}{3}=\frac{12}{3}  
]

[  
x=4  
]

---

# Rule 3: Fractions are division

If:

[  
\frac{x}{4}=5  
]

Multiply both sides by 4:

[  
4\times\frac{x}{4}=5\times4  
]

[  
x=20  
]

This is why people say:

> "4 goes to the other side."

But that's just a shortcut.

What's really happening is multiplying both sides by 4.

---

# Rule 4: Cross multiplication

If:

[  
\frac{x}{4}=\frac{5}{2}  
]

Multiply both sides by 4 and 2:

[  
2x=20  
]

[  
x=10  
]

Shortcut:

[  
x\times2=5\times4  
]

This is called cross multiplication.

---

# Rule 5: Why does a fraction flip?

Suppose:

[  
\frac{3}{4}x=12  
]

To remove (\frac{3}{4}), divide by (\frac{3}{4}):

[  
x=12\div\frac{3}{4}  
]

Dividing by a fraction means multiplying by its reciprocal:

[  
x=12\times\frac{4}{3}  
]

The fraction "flips".

This is one of the most important rules in algebra.

This is the exact algebra rule you're missing.

You are thinking:

> "When something goes to the other side of =, it always changes."

But **it depends on the operation**.

### If it's addition

[  
x l+ 5 = 8  
]

Subtract 5 from both sides:

[  
x = 8 - 5  
]

The sign changes because you're undoing **addition**.

---

### If it's multiplication

Your equation is:

[  
\frac{16}{100}\times\frac{40}{100}\times x = 8  
]

The (\frac{16}{100}) is **multiplying** (x), not adding to it.

To remove it, divide both sides by (\frac{16}{100}):

# [  
\frac{\frac{16}{100}\times\frac{40}{100}\times x}{\frac{16}{100}}

\frac{8}{\frac{16}{100}}  
]

The left side becomes:

[  
\frac{40}{100}x  
]

The right side becomes:

[  
8 \div \frac{16}{100}  
]

Now comes the fraction rule:

> Dividing by a fraction = Multiplying by its reciprocal

# [  
8 \div \frac{16}{100}

8 \times \frac{100}{16}  
]

Notice:

[  
\frac{16}{100}  
]

became

[  
\frac{100}{16}  
]

That's why it **flips upside down**.

---

### Memorize this table

|Original operation|To remove it|
|---|---|
|(+5)|(-5)|
|(-5)|(+5)|
|(\times 5)|(\div 5)|
|(\div 5)|(\times 5)|
|(\times \frac{3}{4})|(\times \frac{4}{3})|

The fraction flips **only because you're dividing by it**.

A good way to think about it:

- Addition ↔ Subtraction
    
- Multiplication ↔ Division
    
- Fraction ↔ Reciprocal (flipped fraction)
    

So in your example, writing

[  
8-\frac{16}{100}  
]

would be wrong because (\frac{16}{100}) was never being **added**. It was being **multiplied**.

Ask yourself each time:

> "Is this number being added, subtracted, multiplied, or divided?"

That tells you what operation to use to remove it.
