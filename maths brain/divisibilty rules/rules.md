**Divisibility rules** are shortcuts used in arithmetic to quickly check whether one number can be divided by another **without actually performing the division**.

Here are the most common ones:

### Basic divisibility rules

- **2** → A number is divisible by 2 if its last digit is even (0, 2, 4, 6, 8).  
    _Example:_ 246 → last digit is 6 → divisible by 2
    
- **3** → Add all digits; if the sum is divisible by 3, the number is too.  
    _Example:_ 123 → 1+2+3 = 6 → divisible by 3
    
- **4** → Look at the last two digits; if they form a number divisible by 4, the whole number is divisible by 4.  
    _Example:_ 316 → last two digits = 16 → divisible by 4
    
- **5** → Ends in 0 or 5.   
    _Example:_ 75 → ends in 5 → divisible by 5
    
- **6** → Must be divisible by both 2 **and** 3.  
    _Example:_ 246 → even and digit sum = 12 → divisible by 6
    
- **7** → A bit trickier rule (not commonly used): double the last digit and subtract it from the remaining number; repeat if needed. If the result is divisible by 7, so is the original number.  
    _Example:_ 203 → 20 − (2×3) = 14 → divisible by 7
    
- **8** → Last three digits must be divisible by 8.  
    _Example:_ 1,024 → 024 = 24 → divisible by 8
    
- **9** → Add all digits; if the sum is divisible by 9, the number is too.  
    _Example:_ 729 → 7+2+9 = 18 → divisible by 9
    
- **10** → Ends in 0.  
    _Example:_ 150 → divisible by 10
    

---

### Why they matter

These rules are very useful in **mental math**, simplifying fractions, factoring numbers, and solving problems faster—especially in exams.
