That's an excellent question.

Strictly speaking, **this is not an official textbook formula** with a standard name. It's a **ratio conversion shortcut** based on percentages.

You can think of it as the:

> **Percentage Conversion Formula** or **Equivalent Percentage Ratio Method**.

These are descriptive names that explain what it's doing, but they are not official mathematical terms.

### The shortcut formula
![[Screenshot 2026-07-02 at 2.00.10 PM.png]]

![[Screenshot 2026-07-02 at 1.29.09 PM 1.png]]
# [  
\boxed{\text{New Value}

\text{Old Value}  
\times  
\frac{\text{New Percentage}}{\text{Old Percentage}}}  
]

---

# When can you use it?

Use it **only when both percentages are based on the same original quantity**.

For example:

### ✅ Profit & Loss

- 20% loss → 20% profit
    

Same Cost Price.

Old SP = 80% of CP

New SP = 120% of CP

# [  
\text{New SP}

\text{Old SP}  
\times\frac{120}{80}  
]

---

### ✅ Salary

Salary increases from 110% to 150%.

Old salary = ₹22,000.

New salary:

[  
22000\times\frac{150}{110}  
]

---

### ✅ Discount

After a 30% discount:

Price = ₹700.

Find the original price.

Old percentage = 70%

New percentage = 100%

[  
700\times\frac{100}{70}  
]

---

### ✅ Tax

Price after adding 18% GST = ₹1180.

Original price?

Old percentage = 118%

New percentage = 100%

[  
1180\times\frac{100}{118}  
]

---

# When should you NOT use it?

❌ If the percentages are based on **different things**.

Example:

- 30% of boys passed.
    
- 40% of girls passed.
    

These percentages are based on **different groups**, so the shortcut doesn't apply.

---

# A quick decision rule

Ask yourself:

> **"Are both percentages referring to the same original value?"**

- ✅ **Yes** → You can use the **percentage conversion shortcut**.
    
- ❌ **No** → Solve each percentage separately.
    

---

## A summary table

|Situation|Base stays the same?|Use the shortcut?|
|---|---|---|
|20% loss → 20% profit (same CP)|✅ Yes|✅ Yes|
|10% increase → 25% increase (same original salary)|✅ Yes|✅ Yes|
|30% discount → original price|✅ Yes|✅ Yes|
|Boys' pass % and girls' pass %|❌ No|❌ No|

### 📌 A name you can remember

For your own notes, I'd suggest writing it as:

> **Percentage Conversion Shortcut (Same Base Rule)**

This reminds you of the most important condition: **the percentages must all refer to the same original quantity (the same base)**. That's why it works.
