### The Apollo Program & Missions to the Moon

The Apollo program was a US human spaceflight program carried out from 1961 to 1972 by NASA, which landed the first astronauts on the Moon. Six missions successfully landed on the Moon, with 12 men walking on the lunar surface and 842 pounds (382 kg) of lunar rocks and soil returned to Earth. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Apollo_missions)

---

#### 🌕 Missions That Reached / Orbited the Moon

|Mission|Date|Key Highlight|
|---|---|---|
|**Apollo 8**|Dec 1968|First crewed spacecraft to orbit the Moon|
|**Apollo 10**|May 1969|Dress rehearsal — orbited Moon, descended to 50,000 ft above surface|
|**Apollo 11**|Jul 1969|**First Moon landing** — Sea of Tranquility|
|**Apollo 12**|Nov 1969|Second landing — Ocean of Storms|
|**Apollo 13**|Apr 1970|Explosion forced flyby — **no landing**|
|**Apollo 14**|Jan 1971|Third landing — Fra Mauro highlands|
|**Apollo 15**|Jul 1971|First use of Lunar Rover|
|**Apollo 16**|Apr 1972|Landing at Descartes highlands|
|**Apollo 17**|Dec 1972|Final Moon mission|

---

#### 🚀 The 6 Successful Moon Landings

**1. Apollo 11 — July 1969** Neil Armstrong, Buzz Aldrin, and Michael Collins made history as Armstrong and Aldrin became the first humans to walk on the Moon. Armstrong's famous words upon landing: _"The Eagle has landed"_, and upon stepping out: _"One small step for man, one giant leap for mankind."_ [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/story/timeline-of-the-apollo-space-missions)

**2. Apollo 12 — November 1969** Pete Conrad, Alan Bean, and Richard Gordon survived two lightning strikes during liftoff and reached the Ocean of Storms, a different spot from Apollo 11. [Space.com](https://www.space.com/apollo-program-overview.html)

**3. Apollo 14 — January 1971** Alan Shepard, Edgar Mitchell, and Stuart Roosa landed in the Fra Mauro formation — the first Moon landing in the lunar highlands. Shepard and Mitchell each spent 9 hours and 35 minutes on the surface. [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/story/timeline-of-the-apollo-space-missions)

**4. Apollo 15 — July 1971** David Scott, James Irwin, and Alfred Worden carried the lunar roving vehicle (moon buggy) to the Moon for the first time. Their mission emphasized geological work. [Space.com](https://www.space.com/apollo-program-overview.html)

**5. Apollo 16 — April 1972** John Young, Charles Duke, and Thomas Mattingly landed at the Descartes highlands searching for volcanic rocks. Confounding scientists' expectations, they found few volcanic samples. [Space.com](https://www.space.com/apollo-program-overview.html)

**6. Apollo 17 — December 1972** The last Apollo mission featured the most extensive lunar exploration of the program, with three moonwalks each lasting more than seven hours while the crew stayed on the Moon for over three days. Commander Gene Cernan and Harrison Schmitt — the first scientist-astronaut to reach the Moon — collected 243 pounds of material. [NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/specials/apollo50th/missions.html)

---

#### ❌ The Notable Failure: Apollo 13

The three astronauts — James Lovell, Fred Haise, and Jack Swigert — survived an oxygen tank explosion. They completed 1.5 orbits around Earth and circled the Moon but never landed. Swigert's famous transmission: _"Houston, we've had a problem."_ [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/story/timeline-of-the-apollo-space-missions)

---

**In total, 24 astronauts flew nine missions to the Moon between December 1968 and December 1972, and 12 men walked on the lunar surface.** [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Apollo_astronauts)

### Artemis II — Humanity's Return to the Moon (2026)

Artemis II has already happened! On April 1, 2026, Artemis II launched on a nearly 10-day voyage around the Moon, marking the first crewed flight of NASA's Orion spacecraft and the first time humans flew around the Moon in over 50 years. [NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/artemis-ii-news-and-updates/)

---

#### 👨‍🚀 The Crew

The crew consisted of NASA astronauts **Reid Wiseman**, **Victor Glover**, and **Christina Koch**, along with **Jeremy Hansen** of the Canadian Space Agency (CSA). [NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/centers-and-facilities/johnson/artemis-ii-mission-milestones-an-image-and-video-recap/)

This was historic — Artemis II carried the first woman (Christina Koch), the first Black man (Victor Glover), and the first Canadian (Jeremy Hansen) to fly around the Moon. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_people_who_have_flown_to_the_Moon)

---

#### 🚀 Mission Details

- Artemis II completed a 10-day journey around the Moon, carrying humanity farther into space than it had gone in over 50 years. The European Service Module powered and sustained Orion throughout the journey, providing propulsion, power, water, and breathable air. [European Space Agency](https://www.esa.int/ESA_Multimedia/Videos/2026/04/Artemis_II_around_the_Moon_in_10_days)
- They passed by the Moon 6,545 km (4,067 miles) from the surface without entering orbit. It was a **circumlunar flyby**, not a landing. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_people_who_have_flown_to_the_Moon)
- Orion completed a 694,481-mile journey around the Moon before reentering Earth's atmosphere and landing in the Pacific Ocean off San Diego on April 10. During reentry, the spacecraft traveled at nearly 35 times the speed of sound. [ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/05/260504023837.htm)
- Orion splashed down with precision, just 2.9 miles from the targeted landing site. [NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/missions/nasa-on-track-for-future-missions-with-initial-artemis-ii-assessments/)

---

#### 🔬 Science on Board

Organ chips containing bone marrow cells from each Artemis II astronaut flew around the Moon with the crew. Researchers are now studying these to determine how deep space radiation and microgravity affect human health at the molecular level. [NASA Science](https://science.nasa.gov/missions/artemis/artemis-2/nasas-artemis-ii-moon-mission-research-continues-on-earth/)

---

#### 🌕 What's Next — Artemis III

NASA is targeting a launch for Artemis III in 2027, with missions to the Moon's surface starting in 2028. After reaching lunar orbit, the crew will transfer from Orion to a commercial lunar lander for their descent to the surface. [NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/artemis-ii-news-and-updates/)

So unlike the Apollo missions, Artemis II did **not land** on the Moon — it was a crewed test flight around it. The actual Moon landing is planned for **Artemis III in 2028**.

Great question! There are several reasons why no humans have landed on the Moon since Apollo 17 in December 1972.

---

#### 💰 1. Enormous Cost

The Apollo program cost roughly **$25 billion** in 1960s money — equivalent to over **$280 billion today**. After achieving the goal of beating the Soviets in the Space Race, the US government had little political will to keep funding such expensive missions. NASA's budget was cut dramatically after Apollo.

---

#### 🏁 2. The Space Race Was Over

Apollo was largely driven by **Cold War competition** with the Soviet Union. Once the US landed on the Moon first, the political motivation was gone. Congress and the public lost interest quickly — even Apollo 17's launch in 1972 was barely watched compared to Apollo 11.

---

#### 🔧 3. No Reusable Hardware

The Saturn V rocket — the most powerful ever built — was **never upgraded or maintained**. The knowledge, tooling, and supply chains were simply **abandoned** after Apollo. Rebuilding that capability from scratch takes enormous time and money, which is essentially what NASA has been doing with the new SLS rocket for decades.

---

#### 🌍 4. Shifting NASA Priorities

After Apollo, NASA focused on:

- **Space Shuttle** (1981–2011) — reusable Earth orbit vehicle
- **International Space Station (ISS)** — long-term human presence in low Earth orbit
- **Mars rovers, Hubble, deep space probes**

The Moon simply wasn't the priority anymore.

---

#### ☢️ 5. The Danger Is Real

Moon missions are extremely dangerous. The Van Allen radiation belts, deep space radiation, micrometeorites, and the Moon's harsh environment (extreme temperature swings from +127°C to -173°C) make every mission a life-or-death undertaking. After Apollo 13's near-disaster, risk tolerance dropped.

---

#### 🛠️ 6. Technology Had to Catch Up

Ironically, going back requires **more advanced** systems than Apollo used — because expectations are higher now. Modern missions require better life support, communication, landing systems, and long-duration surface habitats. These take decades to develop properly.

---

#### 📅 Timeline of Attempts to Return

|Era|Program|Status|
|---|---|---|
|1990s|Various proposals|Cancelled, no funding|
|2004|**Constellation Program** (Bush era)|Cancelled by Obama in 2010|
|2017–present|**Artemis Program**|Finally succeeded — Artemis II flew around Moon April 2026|
|**2028 (planned)**|**Artemis III**|First Moon landing since 1972|

---

#### 🧠 The Short Answer

**Politics + money + loss of urgency.** Apollo happened because of an extraordinary one-time geopolitical crisis (Space Race). Without that pressure, sustaining the political will and funding for Moon missions proved nearly impossible for over 50 years.

Artemis III in 2028 will hopefully end that 56-year gap.
