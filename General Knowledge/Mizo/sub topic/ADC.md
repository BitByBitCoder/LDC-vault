**Autonomous District Councils (ADCs)** are statutory bodies established to provide self-governance to tribal communities in specific regions of India, primarily in the Northeast [1.1.2, 1.2.2].

They are created under the **Sixth Schedule of the Constitution of India** (Articles 244(2) and 275(1)), which grants these councils significant administrative, legislative, and judicial autonomy to protect the distinct social, cultural, and traditional identities of tribal populations [1.1.2, 1.2.1, 1.2.5].

### Key Purposes

The primary objectives of the ADC system include:

- **Preserving Tribal Identity:** Safeguarding the customs, traditions, land rights, and cultural heritage of indigenous tribes [1.2.1, 1.2.2, 1.2.5].
    
- **Decentralized Governance:** Enabling local communities to manage their own affairs, address specific developmental needs, and make decisions without excessive state interference [1.2.1, 1.2.2, 1.2.5].
    
- **Asymmetric Federalism:** Providing a unique institutional mechanism that balances tribal autonomy with national integration [1.2.1].
    

### Powers and Functions

ADCs are empowered to function with three main branches of authority:

- **Legislative:** They can make laws regarding land use, forest management (excluding reserved forests), shifting cultivation, village administration, inheritance of property, marriage, divorce, and social customs [1.1.3, 1.2.1, 1.2.4].
    
- **Executive:** They are responsible for implementing development schemes, managing primary education, health infrastructure, markets, roads, and other local public services [1.1.3, 1.1.4, 1.2.1].
    
- **Judicial:** They can establish village and district council courts to adjudicate disputes between tribal residents based on customary laws (for offenses with a penalty of less than five years imprisonment) [1.1.4, 1.2.1, 1.2.4].
    
- **Financial:** They have the authority to levy and collect certain taxes (e.g., land revenue, taxes on professions, trades, vehicles) and receive grants-in-aid from the central government [1.1.3, 1.2.1, 1.2.4].
    

### ADCs in Mizoram

Mizoram has three Autonomous District Councils, all established in 1972 under the Sixth Schedule [1.1.1, 1.1.2, 1.1.5]:

1. **Chakma Autonomous District Council (CADC)** – Headquarters: Kamalanagar [1.1.2]
    
2. **Lai Autonomous District Council (LADC)** – Headquarters: Lawngtlai [1.1.2, 1.1.5]
    
3. **Mara Autonomous District Council (MADC)** – Headquarters: Siaha [1.1.1, 1.1.2]
    

These councils consist of up to 30 members, the majority of whom are elected by adult suffrage, while a few are nominated by the Governor [1.1.3, 1.2.1, 1.2.2]. While ADCs are vital for local governance, they sometimes face challenges such as limited financial resources, administrative conflicts with state governments, and governance issues like corruption [1.2.1, 1.2.2, 1.2.5].
